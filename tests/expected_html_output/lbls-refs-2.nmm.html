CHAPTER 1

§ 1.A

Introduction

¶ 1.A.1

Some facts about inductively defined structures seem not to admit “straightforward” induction proofs. In such cases, a “non-straightforward” induction proof seems “necessary”. A curious one, or one fond of precision, or anyone with any other motivation, might then wonder whether there is a way to precisely and sensibly state, and prove, that this or that fact does not admit a straightforward induction proof.

¶ 1.A.2

DEFINITIONS

b : ℕ → ℚ
(B)
b(0) ≔ 1
(R)
b(n+1) ≔ b(n)+(n+2)⁻²
¶ 1.A.3

REMARK

Definitions 1.A.2 is by (successor) recursion on the natural numbers.

(a)

Definition 1.A.2(B) is the the base case.

(b)

Definition 1.A.2(R) is the the recursive case.

¶ 1.A.4

FACT

For each n:

b(n) < 2.

¶ 1.A.5

Fact 1.A.4 is a simplification of the Basel problem, which ask for a closed-form expression of the infinite sum:

1 1 1 1
─── + ─── + ─── + ─── + ⋯
1² 2² 3² 4²

Euler (1740) solved this problem and the sum is π²/6 (≈ 1.64).
