Proof by weakened induction hypothesis

Eric Johannesson, eric.johannesson@philosophy.su.se

Anders Lundstedt, anders.lundstedt@philosophy.su.se

ABSTRACT

Sometimes, in order to a prove an arithmetical fact ∀xφ(x) by induction, straightforward induction “does not work”. Instead one “must” use a “stronger” induction hypothesis ψ(x) and prove ∀xψ(x), from which ∀xφ(x) may be derived. This is sometimes called “proof by strengthened induction hypothesis”. We show that this is an instance of a more general phenomenon which we call proof by non-straightforward induction. Cruacially, we show that there are cases where straightforward induction fails, but where proof by incomparable or even weaker induction hypothesis succeeds.

§ 1

Introduction

¶ 1.1

Sometimes, in order to a prove an arithmetical fact ∀xφ(x) by induction, straightforward induction “does not work”. Instead one “must” use a “stronger” induction hypothesis ψ(x) and prove ∀xψ(x), from which ∀xφ(x) may be derived. This is sometimes called “proof by strengthened induction hypothesis”.

¶ 1.2

To give an example, suppose we want to prove that, for all natural numbers n, the sum of the first n odd numbers is a perfect square. Straightforward induction yields the following:

─

Base case: the sum of the first 0 odd numbers is 0, which is a perfect square.

─

Inductive step: if the sum of the first n odd numbers is a perfect square k², then the sum of the first n + 1 odd numbers is

1 + 3 + ... + (2n - 1) + (2n + 1) = k² + 2n + 1

But it is not true that k² + 2n + 1 is a perfect square for all k and n. So we are “stuck”.

¶ 1.3

Instead, we may prove the following more general or stronger result by induction: for all natural numbers n, the sum of the first n odd numbers is n². Straightforward induction yields the following:

─

Base case: the sum of the 0 first odd numbers is 0, which is 0².

─

Inductive step: if the sum of the first n odd numbers is n², then the sum of the first n + 1 odd numbers is n² + 2n + 1 = (n + 1)².

§ 2

Making it precise

¶ 2.1

What does it mean, more precisely, to say that straightforward induction to prove ∀xφ(x) “does not work”, and that we “must” use a “stronger” induction hypothesis and first prove ∀xψ(x)?

Here is how not to characterize the situation (where PA is Peano arithmetic):

(a)
PA ⊬ φ(0) ∧ ∀x(φ(x) → φ(x'))
(b)
PA ⊢ ψ(0) ∧ ∀x(ψ(x) → ψ(x'))
(c)
PA ⊢ ∀xψ(x) → ∀xφ(x)

In fact, this situation is impossible: (b) yields PA ⊢ ∀xψ(x), which by (c) yields PA ⊢ ∀xφ(x), which by pure logic yields

PA ⊢ φ(0) ∧ ∀x(φ(x) → φ(x'))

which contradicts (a).

¶ 2.2

Instead, the situation may perhaps be characterized as follows: starting from the axioms of Peano arithmetic minus the induction axioms, we successively prove more and more theorems using logic and the rule of induction:

φ(0) ∀x(φ(x) → φ(x'))
───────────────────────
∀xφ(x)

Suppose that, at some stage in this process of mathematical inquiry, we have reached a theory T consisting of the axioms and hitherto proved theorems. Then, as we show in § 3 below, a situation may indeed arise satisfying the following definition:

¶ 2.3
DEFINITION

Proof by non-straightforward induction

We say that a theory T and formulas φ(x) and ψ(x) exemplify a case of proof by non-straightforward induction just in case

(a)
T ⊬ φ(0) ∧ ∀x(φ(x) → φ(x'))
(b)
T ⊢ ψ(0) ∧ ∀x(ψ(x) → ψ(x'))
(c)
T ⊢ ∀xψ(x) → ∀xφ(x)
¶ 2.4

FACT

There are extensions T of Robinson arithmetic and formulas φ(x) and ψ(x) exemplifying cases of proof by non-straightforward induction.

¶ 2.5

Perhaps more surprisingly, proof by non-straightforward induction does not really require ψ(x) to be stronger than φ(x). As we show in § 4 below, there are cases of proof by non-straightforward induction where, relative to T, ψ(x) is actually weaker than φ(x).

§ 3

A non-standard model

¶ 3.1

We define a non-standard model M of Robinson arithmetic as follows. Let

A = {..., a[−2], a[−1], a[0] ,a[1] , a[2], ...}

be a countably infinite set disjoint from the natural numbers ℕ, and let the domain of the model be ℕ ∪ A. We shall call A the (set of) non-standard numbers (of M). Let the constant 0 be interpreted as the number 0, and extend the interpretation of the function symbols ', +, and × to the non-standard numbers as follows:

(a)
a[z]' = a[z+1] for z ∈ ℤ.
(b)
a[z] + n = n + a[z] = a[z+n] for z ∈ ℤ and n ∈ ℕ.
(c)
a[z] + a[u] = a[u] + a[z] = a[z+u] for z,u ∈ ℤ.
(d)
a[z] × 0 = 0 × a[z] = 0 for z ∈ ℤ.
(e)
a[z] × n = n × a[z] = a[z × n] for z ∈ ℤ and n ∈ ℕ − {0}.
(f)
a[z] × a[u] = a[u] × a[z] = a[z × u] for z,u ∈ ℤ.

¶ 3.2

It can easily be verified that M is also a model of what we may call minimal arithmetic, which is the theory you get by adding commutativity, associativity and distribution laws for addition and multiplication to the axioms of Robinson arithmetic.

¶ 3.3
PROOF

of Fact 2.4

To verify that cases of proof by non-straightforward induction are indeed possible, it suffices to find true formulas φ(x) and ψ(x) (true in the sense of being satisfied by all natural numbers in the standard model as well as in the non-standard model) with the following profile regarding the non-standard numbers of M:

... a[−2] a[−1] a[0] a[1] a[2] ...
──────────────────────────────────────────────
φ(x) | ... false false true false false ...
ψ(x) | ... false false false false false ...

The induction step will then fail for φ(x) with a[0] assigned to x, but will not fail for ψ(x). Granted as much, we can let T be the theory you get by adding, to minimal arithmetic, the axioms

(a)
ψ(0) ∧ ∀x(ψ(x) → ψ(x'))
(b)
∀xψ(x) → ∀xφ(x)

Since (a) and (b) will be true in M, the model will satisfy T. Moreover, (a) of Definition 2.3 will be satisfied as witnessed by a[0] assigned to x.

For instance, let φ(x) be the formula

∀y∀z(x = x × x ∨ (y + x = z + x → y = z))

and let ψ(x) be the formula

∀y∀z(y + x = z + x → y = z)

Clearly, ψ(x) characterizes the non-standard numbers of M in the sense that they and only they fail to satisfy it. But due to (e) of ¶ 3.1, φ(x) is satisfied by M in the special case of a[0] being assigned to x.

§ 4

Proof by non-strengthened induction hypothesis

¶ 4.1

In the proof of Fact 2.4, ψ(x) was stronger than φ(x), even in the sense that ⊢ ∀x(ψ(x) → φ(x)). Thus, this was a clear case of proof by strengthened induction hypothesis, albeit a contrived one.

But does proof by non-straightforward induction really require a stronger induction hypothesis? No. As a matter of fact, there are cases of what we call proof by incomparable induction hypothesis and proof by weakened induction hypothesis, respectively.

¶ 4.2
DEFINITION

Proof by incomparable induction hypothesis

We say that a theory T and formulas φ(x) and ψ(x) exemplify a case of proof by incomparable induction hypothesis just in case they exemplify a case of proof by non-straightforward induction and, in addition,

(a)
T ⊢ ∀xφ(x) → ∀xψ(x)
(b)
T ⊬ ∀x(ψ(x) → φ(x))
(c)
T ⊬ ∀x(φ(x) → ψ(x))
¶ 4.3

REMARK

By (a) of Definition 4.2 and (c) of Definition 2.3, we have in effect

T ⊢ ∀xφ(x) ↔ ∀xψ(x)

But because of (b) of Definition 4.2 and (c) of Definition 4.2, we may still say that φ(x) and ψ(x) are incomparable relative to T.

¶ 4.4

FACT

There are extensions T of Robinson arithmetic and formulas φ(x) and ψ(x) exemplifying a case of proof by incomparable induction hypothesis.

¶ 4.5

PROOF

To show that proof by incomparable induction hypothesis is possible, it suffices to find true formulas φ(x) and ψ(x) with the following profile regarding the non-standard numbers of M:

... a[−2] a[−1] a[0] a[1] a[2] ...
──────────────────────────────────────────────
φ(x) | ... true true true false false ...
ψ(x) | ... false false true true true ...

For instance, let φ(x) be the formula

∀y(x ≠ y ∧ ∃z(y + z = x) → x × x ≠ y × y)

and let ψ(x) be the formula

∀y(x ≠ y ∧ ∃z(x + z = y) → x × x ≠ y × y)

Observe that, by simple relabeling of variables, we have

(a)
⊢ ∀xφ(x) ↔ ∀xψ(x)

Thus, let T be the theory you get by adding, to minimal arithmetic, the axiom

(b)
ψ(0) ∧ ∀x(ψ(x) → ψ(x'))

Clearly, both φ(x) and ψ(x) are true of all natural numbers in the standard model. To verify that φ(x) has the required profile with respect to M, we need to consider the following two cases:

─

x is assigned a natural number n. Whatever y is assigned that is distinct from n, it will be true in M that x × x ≠ y × y. So φ(x) is true of the natural numbers of M.

─

x is assigned a non-standard number a[i] for some integer i. Then either y is assigned a natural number, in which case M satisfies the antecedent x × x ≠ y × y, or y is assigned a non-standard number a[j] for some integer j ≠ i. By (f) of ¶ 3.1, whenever j < i < 1, M satisfies x × x ≠ y × y. Likewise, whenever i > 0 and j = -i, M satisfies ∃z(y + z = x) and x × x = y × y. Thus, φ(x) is true in M for every non-standard number a[i] such that i < 1, and false whenever i > 0.

By symmetry of reasoning, we see that ψ(x) also has the required profile.

¶ 4.6
DEFINITION

Proof by weakened induction hypothesis

We say that a theory T and formulas φ(x) and ψ(x) exemplify a case of proof by weakened induction hypothesis just in case they they exemplify a case of proof by non-straightforward induction and, in addition,

(a)
T ⊢ ∀xφ(x) → ∀xψ(x)
(b)
T ⊬ ∀x(ψ(x) → φ(x))
(c)
T ⊢ ∀x(φ(x) → ψ(x))
¶ 4.7

REMARK

By (a) of Definition 4.6 and (c) of Definition 2.3, we have in effect

T ⊢ ∀xφ(x) ↔ ∀xψ(x)

But because of (b) of Definition 4.6 and (c) of Definition 4.6, we may still say that ψ(x) is weaker than φ(x) relative to T.

¶ 4.8

FACT

There are extensions T of Robinson arithmetic and formulas φ(x) and ψ(x) exemplifying a case of proof by weakened induction hypothesis.

¶ 4.9

PROOF

To show that proof by weakened induction hypothesis is possible, it suffices to find true formulas φ(x) and ψ(x) with the following profile:

... a[−2] a[−1] a[0] a[1] a[2] ...
──────────────────────────────────────────────
φ(x) | ... false false true false false ...
ψ(x) | ... false false true true true ...

For instance, let φ(x) be the formula

∀y(x ≠ y → x × x ≠ y × y)

and let ψ(x) be the formula

∀y(x ≠ y ∧ ∃z(x + z = y) → x × x ≠ y × y)

Observe that ⊢ ∀x(φ(x) → ψ(x)). It should be clear from Proof 4.5 that the formulas have the required profile. Moreover, with u < v defined as

u ≠ v ∧ ∃z(u + z = v)

we have

(a)
∀x∀y(x < y ∨ x = y ∨ y < x) ⊢ ∀xψ(x) → ∀xφ(x)

To see why, assume that

(b)
∀x∀y(x < y ∨ x = y ∨ y < x)

and

(c)
∀x∀y(x ≠ y ∧ ∃z(x + z = y) → x × x ≠ y × y)

Suppose that x ≠ y. By (b), we have x < y or y < x. In the case of x < y, we have by definition that ∃z(x + z = y). Thus, by (c), we have x × x ≠ y × y, as desired. In the case of y < x, we have by definition that ∃z(y + z = x). By (b), we get by relabeling of variables that

∀y∀x(y ≠ x ∧ ∃z(y + z = x) → y × y ≠ x × x)

Hence, by (c), we get y × y ≠ x × x, which yields the desired result by pure logic.

Lastly, we observe that M satisfies (b). Thus, let T be the theory you get by adding, to minimal arithmetic, the axioms

(d)
∀x∀y(x < y ∨ x = y ∨ y < x)
(e)
ψ(0) ∧ ∀x(ψ(x) → ψ(x'))
¶ 4.10

REMARK

We here have cases where, relative to a base theory T, one must use a different induction hypothesis, and may use one that is not stronger. Are there cases where one both may and must use a non-strengthened induction hypothesis? Indeed there are! (Fact 5.11). This follows from a result reported by Hetzl and Wong in (a) of References, which we expand upon below.

§ 5

Proof by necessarily non-strengthened induction hypothesis

¶ 5.1

It can be shown that, if something is provable in an arithmetical theory containing all the induction axioms, it is provable in that theory using at most one induction axiom. This generalizes Corollary 2.10 reported by Hetzl and Wong.

¶ 5.2

DEFINITION

For each formula Θ(x,z̄), we define the corresponding induction axiom IΘ as the closed formula

∀z̄[θ(0,z̄) ∧ ∀y(θ(y,z̄) → θ(y',z̄)) → ∀xθ(x,z̄)]
¶ 5.3
LEMMA

Generalizing Lemma 2.8 of Hetzl and Wong

Let Θ(x,z̄) be a formula, and define φ(x) as

∀z̄[θ(0,z̄) ∧ ∀y(θ(y,z̄) → θ(y',z̄)) → θ(x,z̄)]

Then we have

(a)
⊢ φ(0) ∧ ∀x(φ(x) → φ(x'))

and

(b)
⊢ ∀xφ(x) ↔ Iθ
¶ 5.4

PROOF

First we show Lemma 5.3(a). We have φ(0) by ∧-elimination, →-introduction and ∀-introduction. To show the second conjunct, assume φ(a), i.e.

(a)
∀z̄[θ(0,z̄) ∧ ∀y(θ(y,z̄) → θ(y',z̄)) → θ(a,z̄)]

Assume also

(b)
θ(0, b̄) ∧ ∀y(θ(y, b̄) → θ(y',b̄))

By (a) and ∀-elimination, we have

θ(0,b̄) ∧ ∀y(θ(y,b̄) → θ(y',b̄)) → θ(a,b̄)

With (b), →-elimination yields Θ(a,b̄). By the second conjunct of (b), this yields Θ(a',b̄). Discharing (b), →-introduction yields

θ(0,b̄) ∧ ∀y(θ(y,b̄) → θ(y',b̄)) → θ(a',b̄)

By ∀-introduction, we get

∀z̄[θ(0,z̄) ∧ ∀y(θ(y,z̄) → θ(y',z̄)) → θ(a',z̄)]

Discharging (a), →-introduction yields ⊢ φ(a) → φ(a'), and ∀-introduction finally yields ⊢ ∀x(φ(x) → φ(x')).

Secondly, we show Lemma 5.3(b). For right to left, assume Iθ, i.e.

(c)
∀z̄[θ(0,z̄) ∧ ∀y(θ(y,z̄) → θ(y',z̄)) → ∀xθ(x,z̄)]

We need to show ∀xφ(x), i.e.

(d)
∀x∀z̄[θ(0,z̄) ∧ ∀y(θ(y,z̄) → θ(y',z̄)) → θ(x,z̄)]

To this end, assume

(e)
θ(0,b̄) ∧ ∀y(θ(y,b̄) → θ(y',b̄))

By (c) and ∀-elimination, we get

θ(0,b̄) ∧ ∀y(θ(y,b̄) → θ(y',b̄)) → ∀xθ(x,b̄)

With (e), →-elimination yields ∀xθ(x,b̄), which by ∀-elimination yields θ(a,b̄). Discharging (e), →-introduction followed by ∀-introduction yields (d).

For left to right, assume (d). We need to show (c). To this end, assume

(f)
θ(0,b̄) ∧ ∀y(θ(y,b̄) → θ(y',b̄))

By (d) and ∀-elimination, we get

θ(0,b̄) ∧ ∀y(θ(y,b̄) → θ(y',b̄)) → θ(a,b̄)

Together with (f) and →-elimination, this yields get θ(a,b̄). By ∀-introduction, this yields ∀xΘ(x,b̄). Discharging (f), →-introduction followed by ∀-introduction yields (c).

¶ 5.5
FACT

Generalizing Proposition 2.9 of Hetzl and Wong

For any formulas θ₁(x,z̄), ..., θₙ(x,z̄), there is a formula φ(x) such that

(a)
⊢ ϕ(0) ∧ ∀x(ϕ(x) → ϕ(x'))

and

(b)
⊢ ∀xϕ(x) ↔ Iθ₁ ∧ ... ∧ Iθₙ
¶ 5.6

PROOF

By Lemma 5.3, there is a formula φₖ(x) for each 1 ≤ k ≤ n such that

(a)
⊢ φₖ(0) ∧ ∀x(φₖ(x) → φₖ(x'))

and

(b)
⊢ ∀xφₖ(x) ↔ Iθₖ

Define φ(x) as φ₁(x) ∧ ... ∧ φₙ(x). Then (a) of Fact 5.5 easily follows from (a). Likewise, (b) of Fact 5.5 follows from (b) and the fact that

⊢ ∀xφ₁(x) ∧ ... ∧ ∀xφₙ(x) ↔ ∀x(φ₁(x) ∧ ... ∧ φₙ(x))
¶ 5.7

FACT

For any formulas θ₁(x,z̄), ..., θₙ(x,z̄), there is a formula φ(x) such that ⊢ Iφ ↔ Iθ₁ ∧ ... ∧ Iθₙ.

¶ 5.8

PROOF

By Fact 5.5, there is a formula φ(x) such that

⊢ φ(0) ∧ ∀x(φ(x) → φ(x'))

and

⊢ ∀xφ(x) ↔ Iθ₁ ∧ ... ∧ Iθₙ

What remains is to observe that ⊢ ∀xφ(x) ↔ Iφ. Left to right follows essentially by ∀-elimination. For right to left, assume Iφ. Then ∀xφ(x) follows by →-elimination.

¶ 5.9
FACT

Generalizing Corollary 2.10 of Hetzl and Wong

Let IND be the set of induction axioms, let T be a set of sentences, and let σ be a sentence. If T + IND ⊢ σ, then there is a formula φ(x) such that T + Iφ ⊢ σ.

¶ 5.10

PROOF

Assume that T + IND ⊢ σ. Then there is a finite set θ₁(x,z̄), ..., θₙ(x,z̄) of formulas such that T + Iθ₁ + ... + Iθₙ ⊢ σ. By Fact 5.7, there is a formula φ(x) such that ⊢ Iφ ↔ Iθ₁ ∧ ... ∧ Iθₙ. Hence, T + Iφ ⊢ σ.

¶ 5.11
FACT

Generalizing Proposition 3.2 of Hetzl and Wong

Assume that T + IND ⊢ σ but T ⊬ σ, and let φ(x) be x = 0 → σ. Then we have

(a)
T + IND ⊢ ∀xφ(x)
(b)
T ⊬ ∀xφ(x)

and there is ψ(x) such that

(c)
T + Iψ ⊢ ∀xφ(x)

Moreover, for any such ψ(x), we have

(d)
T ⊬ ∀x(ψ(x) → φ(x))
¶ 5.12

PROOF

The first part follows by Fact 5.9. For the last part, assume that ψ(x) is a formula of the required kind. Since T ⊬ σ, there is a model M of T such that M ⊭ σ and thus M ⊭ ∀xφ(x). Hence, by (c) of Fact 5.11, we have M ⊭ Iψ, which means that M ⊨ ψ(0) but M ⊭ φ(0). Hence, M ⊭ ψ(0) → ϕ(0), so T ⊬ ∀x(ψ(x) → φ(x)).

¶ 5.13

REMARK

The authors are not aware of any “natural” cases of proof by incomparable or weakened induction hypothesis. Observe that Fact 5.11 does not yield a case where straightforward induction fails in the induction step, since T ⊬ ϕ(0) but T ⊢ ∀x(ϕ(x) → ϕ(x')).
